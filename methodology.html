Methodology

How Cube Earth works

A complete, honest account of our data, methods, validation, and limitations — including approaches we tested and rejected.

Why we're transparent about uncertainty

Cube Earth is designed to support decision-making, not replace it. Every prediction includes a calibrated confidence score, and known limitations are published alongside model performance. When confidence is low, we recommend treating predictions as evidence for further review rather than definitive conclusions.

Dataset

Our production model is trained and validated on 61,866 tillage parcels drawn from Ireland's Land Parcel Identification System (LPIS) 2024 dataset, published by the Department of Agriculture, Food and the Marine (DAFM) as open data.

The eight tillage crop types are: Spring and Winter Barley, Spring and Winter Wheat, Spring and Winter Oats, Maize, and Rye. The LPIS CROP field serves two purposes in our system: as the ground-truth label for training, and as the "farmer declared" value we compare our satellite prediction against.

Satellite data source

We use Sentinel-2 Level-2A optical imagery, part of the European Space Agency's Copernicus programme, accessed via Element84's public AWS catalogue. This data is free, updated approximately every 5 days (cloud cover permitting), and requires no licensing for commercial use.

For each of Ireland's 18 Sentinel-2 tiles (MGRS grid squares), we select the lowest-cloud-cover scene available each month, across four full growing seasons (2022–2025), and extract reflectance values at each parcel's location.

Feature engineering

From the raw satellite reflectance bands, we compute three vegetation indices for each parcel, at 32 points across each year (approximately 11-day intervals, or "dekads"):

IndexFormulaWhat it measures
NDVI(NIR − Red) / (NIR + Red)General vegetation greenness and density
NDRE(NIR − Red Edge) / (NIR + Red Edge)Chlorophyll content, sensitive to crop stress
NDII(NIR − SWIR) / (NIR + SWIR)Canopy water content

Missing observations (due to cloud cover on a given date) are filled by linear interpolation across the year — verified to leave 0% of data points unfilled in our production dataset, so no artificial default values enter the model.

Model training

We use CatBoost, a gradient-boosted decision tree algorithm, trained as a multi-class classifier across the 8 crop types. Because Spring Barley alone accounts for over half of all tillage parcels (33,346 of 61,866 — a 65:1 ratio against the smallest class, Rye), we apply explicit class-weighting during training to prevent the model from simply defaulting to the majority class.

We tested this choice rather than assuming it: an identical Random Forest model, trained on the same features, achieved only 53.9% balanced accuracy compared to CatBoost's 71.7% — confirming that CatBoost's class-weighting handles this imbalance meaningfully better.

Balanced accuracy methodology

We report balanced accuracy rather than overall accuracy, because overall accuracy can be misleading when one class dominates the dataset. Balanced accuracy averages the recall (correct identification rate) across all 8 crop types equally, so performance on minority crops like Rye or Winter Oats counts as much as performance on Spring Barley.

Confidence calibration

Raw model output confidence is not automatically trustworthy — many machine learning models are systematically over- or under-confident. We tested our model's raw confidence against actual outcomes and found it was under-confident: at the 60–70% displayed confidence range, actual accuracy was 79.4%, a 14.5-point gap.

We corrected this using isotonic regression, fit on a held-out validation set, mapping raw confidence to true reliability without changing which crop is predicted. After calibration, the gap between displayed confidence and actual accuracy is under 4 percentage points across all confidence ranges — the confidence score shown on every parcel is a genuine estimate of reliability, not a marketing number.

Known limitations

Spring cereal confusion. Spring Barley, Spring Oats, and Spring Wheat have overlapping growing seasons and similar spectral signatures, particularly around mid-March. These three crops are more often confused with each other than with Maize or Winter crops, which have more distinct growth patterns.

Cloud cover sensitivity. Satellite observations are affected by cloud cover. Prediction confidence may decrease for parcels or time periods where limited clear imagery was available.

Tillage crops only. The current model covers 8 tillage crop types. Grassland, permanent pasture, and other land uses are not yet classified.

Model estimates, not field-verified dates. Growth stage and harvest window predictions are derived from the crop classification and typical phenology, not from direct field observation.

Data density varies by year. 2024 had denser, cleaner satellite coverage than other years in our dataset, and the model relies on it somewhat more heavily — a known artefact of data availability, not an intentional design choice.

Why SAR (radar) was not used in the production model

Synthetic Aperture Radar (Sentinel-1) is often assumed to improve crop classification, since it works through cloud cover. We tested this assumption directly rather than assuming it, across five distinct architectures:

Raw SAR features, early fusion (combined with optical)

Balanced accuracy dropped from 74.6% to approximately 69–74%, depending on configuration.

Zonal polygon SAR statistics, early fusion

Balanced accuracy dropped significantly, from 73.7% to 64.9%.

Late fusion (independent SAR + optical models, blended)

Balanced accuracy remained flat at 73.6–73.7% regardless of blend weighting between 0% and 50% SAR. SAR alone achieved only 12.7% (near-random for 8 classes).

L-band SAR (NISAR / ALOS-2 PALSAR-2)

Not extracted after desk research. Published research confirms L-band HV polarisation requires 100m spatial resolution to achieve useful accuracy — incompatible with Irish field sizes, which average 4–8 hectares (roughly 200–280m per side).

We also evaluated higher-resolution commercial optical imagery (PlanetScope, 3m resolution) as an alternative, but were unable to obtain data access within the constraints of available trial and research-licensing programmes, all of which either required university/federal-research affiliation or explicitly prohibited commercial product development.

Conclusion: for Irish dry-land cereal classification, Sentinel-2 optical data alone, with the feature engineering and calibration described above, represents the best-validated approach given freely available, commercially-usable data sources.
